When my inbox is overflowing and my calendar is packed with back-to-back meetings, my body reacts before my mind even registers the stress. My shoulders creep up toward my ears, my jaw locks tight enough to cause a headache, and my breathing becomes shallow. I used to think I didn’t have time to “de-stress,” but I eventually realized that working while physically coiled like a spring isn’t efficient—it’s a recipe for burnout.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) is my go-to tool because it doesn’t require a yoga mat, quiet music, or a change of clothes. You can do it while sitting in your office chair, waiting for a document to print, or even during a conference call where your camera is off.

Why This Works (And Why It Isn’t Just “Relaxing”)

Most people try to fight stress by “thinking” their way out of it. They tell themselves to calm down, which rarely works because stress is a physiological event, not just a mental one.

PMR works by hacking your nervous system. By deliberately tensing a muscle group and then abruptly releasing that tension, you force your body to recognize the difference between “contracted” and “relaxed.” When you release, your muscles have to drop to a state lower than their baseline tension. It’s like hitting a reset button on your physical posture.

The 3-Minute Routine: A Step-by-Step Guide

You can do this entire sequence in three minutes. Sit with your feet flat on the floor and your hands resting on your lap. Close your eyes if you’re in a private space, or just lower your gaze if you’re at your desk.

1. The Hands and Forearms (30 seconds) Make tight fists with both hands. Squeeze until you feel the tension in your knuckles and your forearms. Hold that squeeze for five seconds—really feel the strain. Then, suddenly open your fingers wide and let your hands go limp on your lap. Feel the tingle of blood flow returning to your palms.

2. The Shoulders and Neck (30 seconds) Shrug your shoulders up toward your ears as hard as you can. Scrunch your neck down until you feel the tightness in your trapezius muscles. Hold for five seconds. On the exhale, drop your shoulders down as far as they will go. Let them hang heavy, like they are being pulled toward the floor by weights.

3. The Jaw and Face (30 seconds) Clench your teeth slightly and squeeze your eyes shut. Scrunch your nose as if you’ve just smelled something sour. Hold this facial tension for five seconds. Then, release everything. Let your jaw hang slightly open and let your eyelids rest softly. You’ll be surprised at how much tension you were holding in your forehead without realizing it.

4. The Core and Legs (60 seconds) Tighten your stomach muscles as if you are bracing for a punch, and at the same time, push your heels into the floor to engage your calves and thighs. Hold this full-body tension for five seconds. Release everything at once. Feel your body sink deeper into your chair. Spend the final 30 seconds simply noticing how your breath has changed—it’s likely deeper and slower now.

How to Make It Stick During a Busy Day

The biggest mistake people make with PMR is waiting until they reach a breaking point to try it. By then, your cortisol levels are already peaking, and it’s harder to get the physical “reset” to take hold.

Instead, I use “anchor points” to remind myself to practice. Here are a few triggers I use to weave this into a standard workday:

  • The Transition Trigger: Perform the routine every time you finish a Zoom call and before you open the next email. It acts as a mental divider between tasks.
  • The Waiting Trigger: If you are waiting for a program to load or for a file to download, do just the “Hands and Forearms” portion.
  • The Bathroom Stall Method: If you work in an open office and need a moment of privacy, the three-minute routine is the perfect way to get a quick physical refresh before heading back to your desk.
  • The Coffee Break: Use the time while your coffee is brewing or your tea is steeping to complete the full four-step sequence.

Troubleshooting Your Routine

If you find that your muscles feel shaky or overly tense after you release them, don’t worry—that’s actually a sign that you were holding more tension than you realized. Just slow down the release. Instead of letting go all at once, release your muscles in two smaller stages.

If you find your mind wandering back to your “to-do” list, acknowledge the thought and gently bring your focus back to the specific muscle group you are working on. This isn’t meditation; you don’t need to clear your mind. You just need to focus on the physical sensation of the squeeze and the release.

The goal here isn’t to be perfectly calm for the rest of the day. The goal is to provide your body with a brief, high-impact circuit breaker. By repeating this throughout the day, you prevent the “staircase effect,” where your stress levels climb higher and higher with every hour that passes.

Once you have mastered the physical sensation of these four areas, you will start to notice when you are tensing up throughout the day. You’ll catch yourself gripping the mouse too hard or holding your breath before you even feel the mental frustration. That self-awareness is the ultimate tool for long-term stress management.

As you look at your schedule for the rest of the week, identify one or two moments where you can reliably insert this routine. Small, frequent resets are far more effective than trying to “fix” your stress levels with a long, intense session once you’ve already crashed.
